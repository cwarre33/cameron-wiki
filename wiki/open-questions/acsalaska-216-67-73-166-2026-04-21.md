# Responsible Disclosure — Shared Integrator Endpoint `216.67.73.166` (Anchorage AK)

## Summary

A single public IP — `216.67.73.166` — appears as a persistent BACnet Foreign Device Table (FDT) entry on **two different internet-facing Delta Controls BBMDs** operating in Anchorage, Alaska. Both buildings are on the same ACS (Alaska Communications) static block and both run Delta Controls V3.40 on public IPs. One of the two buildings is **100% leased to CenturyLink / Lumen**, a major US telecommunications carrier.

The shared endpoint is almost certainly a **single Building Management System integrator's remote-monitoring station**, bridging from a public ACS static IP into the BACnet networks of at least two client buildings simultaneously. Identifying the owner of that endpoint gets us one disclosure contact whose remediation fixes both buildings.

**All data is from Shodan's passive index. No systems were probed, accessed, or interacted with.**

**What this pattern means.** A single public IP is maintaining persistent BACnet Foreign Device registrations with two different building BBMDs for 47+ days. Both registrations show the same rotating-port behavior (13 distinct ephemeral ports each), strongly indicating a long-running client process on the far side of a NAT that re-binds on every re-registration. The persistence and parallelism make coincidence implausible — this is one operator running a monitoring station that bridges into two client buildings. That is, by definition, an **integrator pattern**.

### Why this matters beyond "two buildings exposed"

1. **Shared-endpoint discovery is a leverage multiplier.** One integrator disclosure fixes N buildings. Our seed set found one such endpoint in 17 BBMDs — the true population may be an order of magnitude larger. See [[bacnet-fdt-external-tunnels-2026-04-21]].
2. **One of the buildings houses a major US telecom tenant.** 6411 A Street in Anchorage is 100% leased to CenturyLink/Lumen. BACnet write access to that building's HVAC, lighting, and access-adjacent systems is a supply-chain-adjacent exposure for a Tier-1 carrier facility. ⚠️ Whether the BACnet network reaches any telecom-infrastructure-critical systems (BDFB rooms, battery plant cooling, MDF environmental) vs. only tenant-office comfort HVAC cannot be determined passively.
3. **The integrator is the single point of remediation.** Even if both building operators are notified, if the integrator's monitoring configuration is the upstream cause, the problem returns the moment either site re-activates remote monitoring.

## Recommended disclosure message (integrator-first draft)

> **Subject:** Responsible Disclosure — Internet-Reachable BACnet Monitoring Endpoint (216.67.73.166)
>
> I am a security researcher conducting passive OSINT analysis of internet-exposed building automation systems. Using only data already indexed by Shodan (no active scanning, no probing, no interaction with any systems), I identified a pattern that points to your infrastructure.
>
> **What was observed:**
> Two internet-facing Delta Controls BBMDs in Anchorage (`24.237.132.230` / "Huffman Plaza 12350 Industry Way" and `66.58.248.125` / "6411 A Street") both have `216.67.73.166` as a persistent Foreign Device Table entry. Across Shodan's history, both registrations have been continuous since 2026-03-04 (47+ days) with rotating source ports consistent with a long-running client process. `216.67.73.166` resolves as `216-67-73-166.static.acsalaska.net` — an ACS static commercial assignment in Anchorage, which is the profile of an integrator's remote-monitoring station.
>
> **What I would like you to verify:**
> 1. That `216.67.73.166` is an endpoint you operate (monitoring station, gateway appliance, or similar).
> 2. That the remote-access architecture into both client buildings is intentional.
> 3. That the BACnet Foreign Device registration is authenticated / VPN-gated at some layer other than the BACnet protocol itself (BACnet has no authentication).
> 4. That the two building BBMDs are **intended** to be reachable from the public internet on UDP 47808 (I believe this is unintentional — the current configuration lets any BACnet client on the internet enumerate and write to both buildings).
>
> **Recommended immediate actions:**
> 1. Block UDP 47808 inbound at the perimeter firewall at both client sites — this removes the internet-exposure immediately.
> 2. Move all remote monitoring behind a VPN (site-to-site IPsec or WireGuard) between your monitoring station and each client's BBMD.
> 3. Audit Foreign Device registrations at both client sites and confirm only your managed endpoint(s) are present.
> 4. Provide each client with a brief describing the architectural fix and any evidence of unauthorized BACnet reads or writes in their controller logs.
>
> I have no intention of accessing, testing, or interacting with any of these systems. I am disclosing this in good faith and am available to provide technical detail to your engineering team and to coordinate with CISA ICS-CERT and the building tenants if you prefer.
